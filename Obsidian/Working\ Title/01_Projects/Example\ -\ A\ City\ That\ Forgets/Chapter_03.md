# Chapter 03: The Return

Three weeks into the trial, Maya woke without reaching for her note.

This alone was new. The reaching had been automatic as breathing. But this morning, the room assembled itself in her mind with its full history attached. The cream-colored walls. The window that didn't open all the way. The man who'd been not-her-husband for two years—she could feel that duration, could taste the specific texture of two years of waking beside someone you didn't remember agreeing to.

And Zara. She could hold Zara in her mind the way other people held memories: not perfectly, but held.

She made coffee instead of checking the book. This was foolish, maybe. Overconfident. But she wanted to know if the compound was working or if she was just having a good morning. Everyone had good mornings sometimes.

Zara was already awake, at the kitchen table with her phone. When Maya came in, Zara looked up, and something fractured in her face.

"You remembered my name," Zara said.

"Zarah."

"Zara. Just Zara."

"I know." And she did. She understood the correction in the same way she understood the question underneath it—*do you really?*

They drank coffee together for the first time in months, maybe years. The compound had given her enough back to know she'd been gone. It was a strange gift: remembering both the forgetting and the remembered. She could feel the architecture of their relationship, the load-bearing walls of it, even if some of the details were still missing.

"Are you going to keep taking it?" Zara asked.

"I don't know. Ask me tomorrow." She meant it as a joke, but Zara didn't laugh. "I'll leave you a note," Maya added, quieter. "I always do. That part doesn't change."

Zara reached across the table and took her hand. "You're already changing," she said. "That's the part that scares me."

Maya didn't know what to say. The trial had promised to make her less of a ghost, but Zara was right—the person coming back was not the person who'd left. Some distances, Maya was learning, you couldn't travel twice.

That evening, she wrote in the blue book:

*Today I remembered Zara's name without looking. I remembered two years. The compound is working, but Zara seems sadder, not happier. I don't know if that's because I'm becoming someone new, or because I'm finally seeing what I've put her through. Tomorrow I'll keep taking the pills. If I remember this, I'll make the same choice again.*

She closed the book and left it on the nightstand. Outside, the city was settling into dusk. Somewhere in the buildings around them, people were taking their notes, preparing for the forgetting, living their lives in one-day increments. Maya was caught between that world and another one—remembering, but not enough. Changed, but not whole.

She left the book where her daughter could find it. In this city, that was the closest thing to faith.
