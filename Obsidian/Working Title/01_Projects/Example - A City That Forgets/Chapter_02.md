# Chapter 02: The Trial

Dr. Reeves' office smelled like ammonia. Maya sat in a chair that was too small, her hands folded in her lap like a patient who had forgotten she was a nurse.

"The trial is voluntary," Reeves said. He had kind eyes. She'd noted that in her book: *eyes you can trust.* But trust was a luxury in this city. "We're testing a compound that helps consolidate memories. It won't give you back what's already gone. But it might slow the forgetting."

"How much slower?"

"That's what we're measuring. Some people report remembering most of a day. Others remember fragments."

"And the side effects?"

Reeves glanced away. "Some nausea. Vivid dreams. In the early trials, a few people reported... distress. Being forced to remember things they'd rather forget."

Maya thought of Zara's torn note. "Who takes it?"

"People like you. People who can't afford to lose any more."

She'd come here because she had to. Because her job at Riverside required continuity—you couldn't give medications to patients whose names you'd forgotten by noon. Because Zara needed a mother who knew her. But reading his face, Maya understood something else. He needed people to take the trial. The city needed the cure to work.

"If I say yes," she said, "do I still have my notes? Or does the trial mean—"

"You can keep taking notes," Reeves interrupted. "Most of our volunteers do. It's a backup system, isn't it? If the compound works, you'll have both. If it doesn't—" He shrugged. The shrug meant *at least you'll have something.*

That night, Maya made two entries in her morning book. One was the decision: *I took the trial. Dr. Reeves' office, Tuesday. I'm trying.*

The second was for Zara. She wrote it while Zara was asleep, pressing hard into the page so the letters would be clear.

*I'm sorry. I know this is hard. But I'm going to remember you now. I promise.*

When she reread it, Maya cried. She didn't know which was worse—the forgetting itself, or the fact that she couldn't even remember what it felt like to be her daughter's mother. The notes were all she had. The notes, and this strange compound now working through her bloodstream, and the hope that it would be enough.
