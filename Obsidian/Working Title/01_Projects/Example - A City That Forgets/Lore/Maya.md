# Maya

**Age:** Early 40s (implied)  
**Occupation:** Nurse at Riverside Hospital  
**Household:** Apartment 417, south wing. Lives with daughter Zara and an unnamed partner ("the not-husband").

## Voice & Speech Markers

When writing notes, Maya uses a formal, terse register. Staccato sentences. No elaboration. When speaking to herself or in interior monologue, she is more reflective. In dialogue with Zara and Reeves, she shifts to questions and careful listening.

- **Vocabulary:** Clinical when professional (her notes, her job language). Introspective when alone.
- **Sentence structure:** Fragmented in notes, longer and more complex in introspection.
- **Contraction use:** Rare in notes (formality), frequent in dialogue.
- **Recurring constructions:** "I am X" statements to define herself. Questions about what she should trust.

## Key relationships

- **Zara:** Her daughter. The relationship is damaged by her forgetting; by Ch. 03, Maya is beginning to remember, but Zara "seems sadder, not happier" as a result of seeing her mother change.
- **The unnamed partner:** A source of domestic confusion. Maya wakes next to him with no memory of choosing him. He's present as absence: "the man in 418 is not my husband."
- **Dr. Reeves:** Authority figure offering medical intervention. She trusts him cautiously, notes that his eyes are trustworthy, but senses his investment in the trial's success.

## Arc

Maya begins the story as a ghost in her own life, held together entirely by written notes. By Ch. 03, the trial is working; she is consolidating memories. But this "success" brings a new pain: she must now remember both the love and the damage. The story ends with her still between two worlds—remembering, but not whole.
