# Zara

**Age:** 14  
**Household:** Apartment 417, south wing, with Maya (mother) and unnamed cohabitant.

## Voice & Speech Markers

Zara's voice is direct and emotionally present. When distressed, she uses fragments and scratched-out words. When she's trying to be brave (like telling Maya she's "just a client"), her register becomes more formal and distant. She's capable of unusual maturity for her age.

- **Vocabulary:** Contemporary, age-appropriate. Simple words for big emotions.
- **Sentence structure:** Fragments when scared. Longer sentences when explaining or defending.
- **Contraction use:** Heavy (natural for a teenager).
- **Recurring constructions:** Questions that are really pleas ("Mom, there's something wrong. Please go to the doctor.").

## Key relationships

- **Maya:** Her mother. Zara has been bearing the emotional weight of Maya's forgetting—having to re-establish the relationship every day. By Ch. 03, she's terrified of the change that remembering represents. "You're already changing. That's the part that scares me."
- **The unnamed cohabitant:** Not directly addressed by Zara.

## Arc

Zara begins as a scared child doing emotional labor (the torn note, the lie about being "just a client"). By Ch. 03, she's witnessing her mother's return—but it brings a new fear: that the person coming back won't be the person she needed. Her last line ("That's the part that scares me") shifts the story's focus from Maya's hope to the cost of recovery for the people who've adapted to absence.

## Notes

Zara's intelligence and emotional articulation exceed her age. This may be because her situation forced it, or it may be thematic—in a city defined by loss, even teenagers learn to carry adult weight. Consider whether this is sustainable for longer work, or if it risks becoming sentimentality.
